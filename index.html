Days 1–3 · Tokyo

The City That Never Slows Down

You land in Tokyo and step into a city that moves at a completely different speed. Your hotel is in Shibuya or Shinjuku, right in the middle of it all. Drop your bags, head outside, and just walk. The first night is yours to wander. Find a tiny ramen counter with eight seats, order a cold Asahi (trust us on this one), and let the jet lag fade.

Over the next two full days, you explore. Shibuya Crossing. Harajuku for the street style. Shinjuku for the energy and the bars tucked behind unmarked doors. Tsukiji Outer Market for breakfast sushi so fresh it changes everything you thought you knew about fish. Meiji Jingu shrine for a moment of quiet in the middle of the chaos. Akihabara if you want to see the other side of Tokyo.

We will put together a detailed day-by-day guide with restaurant reservations, suggested routes, and tips on navigating the trains (they're easier than you think). This is your time to fall in love with Japan before the main event.

Stylish

Boutique Hotel

A design-forward hotel in Shibuya or Shinjuku with great views, walking distance to nightlife, restaurants, and transit. Think Shibuya Stream, Granbell, or similar. $200–300/night.

Classic

Modern Japanese Hotel

Clean, comfortable, well-located. Hotels like JR Kyushu Blossom or Hotel Century Southern Tower. Great value, reliable quality, easy access to everything. $150–250/night.

Day 4 · Kyoto

Into the Ancient Capital

You board the Shinkansen (bullet train) at Tokyo Station. Two hours and fifteen minutes later, you're in Kyoto. The pace shifts immediately. Narrow streets, wooden machiya townhouses, the smell of incense drifting from temple gates.

Your ryokan is waiting. A traditional Japanese inn with tatami floors, sliding paper screens, and a private onsen (hot spring bath) in your room. You change into yukata robes, soak in the mineral water, and let the anticipation build. Tomorrow is your day.

That evening, walk to a nearby restaurant for kaiseki (a traditional multi-course meal). Every dish is a small work of art. This is the dinner before the dinner, a quiet night for the two of you before everything happens.

Traditional

Central Kyoto Ryokan

A ryokan in the Higashiyama or Gion area with private onsen and breakfast included. Walking distance to temples and the elopement location. Intimate, quiet, and deeply Japanese. $200–350/night.

Secluded

Arashiyama Ryokan

Stay near the bamboo grove and the Katsura River. A more rural, peaceful feel. Slightly further from central Kyoto but closer to nature and some of the most photogenic spots in the city. $200–400/night.

Day 5 · Elopement Day

The Day You've Been Waiting For

Your morning starts with hair and makeup at the ryokan. Our stylist understands Western preferences (this matters more than you think, and Juri makes sure of it). You put on the dress you picked out with your mom and sister. Christian gets ready in the next room.

Your photographer and videographer arrive. From this point, they're with you for the rest of the day, capturing everything in both stills and cinematic video.

You head to your ceremony location. The two of you exchange vows, exchange rings, and share a private moment that belongs to nobody else. No audience. No performance. Just the words you wrote for each other.

After the ceremony, you change into traditional kimonos. Your photographer takes you through Kyoto: temple gardens, quiet backstreets, the bamboo grove if you choose it. Every frame is a mix of Japanese tradition and the two of you being completely yourselves.

The day ends with a celebration dinner at one of Kyoto's best restaurants. Just the two of you, incredible food, and the feeling that you just did something extraordinary.

Ceremony Setting

Cultural

Temple Garden

A private ceremony in the garden of a Kyoto Zen temple. We work with Shunko-in, a sub-temple within the Myoshinji compound — moss, stone lanterns, raked gravel, and centuries of quiet history surrounding you. Permits and coordination arranged by us.

Intimate

Private Machiya

A traditional Kyoto townhouse reserved exclusively for you. Tatami floors, sliding shoji screens, a private garden. Your vows said in complete privacy, exactly the way you want them.

Scenic

Bamboo Grove

Exchange vows surrounded by towering bamboo in Arashiyama. Best done early morning when the light filters through the canopy and the crowds haven't arrived. Unforgettable in photos.

Days 6–7 · Kyoto & Osaka

Explore at Your Own Pace

Day 6 is yours to explore Kyoto as a married couple. Walk through the Fushimi Inari gates. Wander Gion and spot a geisha disappearing around a corner. Visit the golden Kinkaku-ji. Sit in a tea house and try a matcha ceremony. We will give you a curated guide with our favorite spots, but the day is unstructured by design. You just got married. Go where the day takes you.

Day 7, take the 15-minute train to Osaka. This is Japan's food capital, and your mission is simple: eat everything. Takoyaki (octopus balls) from a street stall in Dotonbori. Okonomiyaki (savory pancakes) at a counter where they cook it in front of you. Kushikatsu (fried skewers) with an ice-cold beer. Osaka is louder, messier, and more fun than anywhere else in Japan. You'll love it.

You return to Kyoto that evening for your last night in the ryokan. One more soak in the onsen before the next chapter of the trip begins.

Days 8–9 · Hakone / Kawaguchiko

Your Honeymoon in the Mountains

From Kyoto, you take the bullet train east toward the Mt. Fuji area. Depending on which option you choose, you arrive in either Hakone (a hot spring resort town in the mountains) or Kawaguchiko (a lake town at the base of Mt. Fuji with direct views of the mountain).

This is the wind-down. No itinerary, no schedule, no pressure. You check into a ryokan with a private open-air onsen overlooking the mountains or the lake. You spend two days soaking, eating multi-course meals in your room, taking walks through volcanic valleys or along the lakeshore, and doing absolutely nothing if that's what feels right.

If the weather cooperates, you wake up to Mt. Fuji framed in your window. Even if it doesn't, the quiet and the hot springs make this the perfect way to close the trip before heading home.

Mountain Views

Hakone

Classic hot spring resort town. Volcanic valleys, art museums, a pirate ship on a lake. Only 1 hour from Tokyo for easy departure. Ryokans here have some of the best private onsen in Japan. $200–400/night.

Fuji Views

Kawaguchiko

Right at the base of Mt. Fuji. Wake up to the iconic silhouette reflected in the lake. Quieter and more remote than Hakone. The postcard view you've seen a hundred times, except now it's real. $200–350/night.

Day 10 · Departure

Head Home

From the Fuji area, it's a short train ride back to Tokyo. Depending on your flight time, you might have a few hours to grab a final bowl of ramen, pick up souvenirs, or just sit in a park and take it all in before heading to the airport.

You fly home married, with photos and video that capture exactly what this trip was. And when you're ready, you throw the party back in Charlotte.
